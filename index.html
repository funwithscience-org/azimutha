Azimutha.FE

An anonymous TikTok account publishing documents formatted as research papers — attributed only to “Independent Research,” hosted nowhere, citing nothing checkable. The author invited an audit; here is one. The series is now complete: all thirteen papers reviewed, every claim checked against primary sources you can verify yourself.

The Pattern

What the thirteen papers add up to

Read one at a time, the papers look like thirteen different arguments — about clocks, gyroscopes, Kepler's orbits, the Cavendish experiment, the graviton. Read together, they are one argument, made thirteen times.

Nearly every paper runs on a single move: if science cannot yet state the physical cause of something, that something is a fiction — a “mathematical ghost,” a “legal fiction,” a “calibration target.” The move is never applied evenly. It is aimed only at the forces and particles the author has already decided to reject, and never at the ones his own model needs. Two papers vary the formula rather than repeat it — earth-shapes is a strawman that concedes the mainstream shape outright, and plant-gravity argues empirical biology — but the reflex is the same.

That is why every review ends the same way — not with an appeal to authority, but with an instrument the author himself trusts. He does not doubt magnetism, whose cause was described seven centuries after the compass. He accepts the inverse-square law for light while calling the identical law for gravity a fiction. He venerates E = hv while denying the photon that equation counts. He uses a Faraday cage in one paper to explain away gravity in another. His gravimeters are trustworthy when they drill foundations and corrupt when they measure gravity. Each paper is refuted by a tool from the paper beside it.

Under several of the papers sits one physics point worth stating plainly. The inverse-square law — the thing he grants for light and denies for gravity — is not a claim about light, or gravity, or any mechanism at all. It is a fact about three-dimensional space: anything spread from a point over the surface of a growing sphere thins as one over distance squared, because that is how the area of a sphere grows. Light, sound, an electric field and a gravitational field share the exponent because they share the geometry, not because they share a cause. There is no coherent place to accept it for one and deny it for the other.

The positive model fares no better than the demolition. Rename the downward force — call it density, electrostatics, or “frequency,” as the papers variously do — and a real pull that holds the oceans and weakens with altitude still needs an acceleration term, a g, to exist at all. The replacements the capstone offers only hide it: buoyant sorting is Archimedes' ρgV, atmospheric “downward pressure” is ρgh — the same g he abolished, tucked inside the force meant to replace it.

And the papers do not always agree with each other. The aether is a mainstream embarrassment in one paper and, under another name, the light-carrying medium his own optics require in the next; light travels as an expanding wavefront in one paper and only appears to expand in another; his tired-light cosmology needs the vast intergalactic distances his own dome deletes. The pattern is consistent: the further the papers move from “your instrument doesn't prove causation” toward “here is what the universe actually is,” the more they collide with one another.

One fact holds across all thirteen: the author reports no measurement of his own — no apparatus, no data, no experiment performed — only argument about other people's work, under a demand that everyone else be “hardware-first.” None of which makes the papers worthless to read. The history in them is frequently accurate, the prose is fluent, and the stated instinct — prefer what can be measured to what can only be asserted — is a good one. The papers fail on their own standard, and that is the most honest way to answer them: not from authority, but from the measurements the author already trusts.

The Reviews

Thirteen papers, grouped

The papers fall into three families. Each review follows the same method — steelman first, then claim-by-claim against primary sources — and every one links its verbatim source and its citations.

The instrument teardowns

His most disciplined papers, and his strongest, because they assert the least: each takes one instrument and argues it measures its own behaviour, not the thing it reports. The recurring problem — the instruments supposedly measuring nothing all agree with each other, and with instruments built on unrelated physics.

Reviewed July 2026 · 20 slides Reject

“Gravimeter Analysis” (the ‘Gravitimeter Dissection’)

Tables every gravimeter type and dismisses each with one move: the displacement “could equally be electromagnetic.” That's a testable hypothesis — charge the mass, swap the material, shield the instrument — and physics has bounded the material-dependence to parts in 10¹⁵, far below anything that could imitate gravity. Also: the paper's own numbering runs its cross-cutting failures 1, 4, 5 — its own Failures 2 and 3 were never uploaded.
Reviewed July 2026 · 15 pages · the “teardown” Reject

“A Critical Analysis of… Precision Gravitational Metrology” (the “Cavendish Torsion Balance Dissection”)

Says conflicting measurements of G prove gravity was never isolated, and density-buoyancy replaces it. But here's the scale it never shows: every modern lab lands on 6.674×10⁻¹¹ — the famous disagreement is a ~0.05% fight over the fourth digit, not a force that reads differently day to day. And every specific objection — the fiber's memory, the turntable's vibration, the “circular” software — is answered in the labs' own published words, including a cold-atom measurement with no torsion balance at all. Mass-attraction was measured with a plumb bob 24 years before Cavendish.
Reviewed July 2026 · 17 slides Reject

“A Critical Examination of Gyroscope Manufacturing…”

Twelve gyroscope types, each declared uniquely blind to Earth's 15°/h rotation while trusted for every other rotation it reports — a device can't tell the two apart. The error budgets are real; the engineers who wrote them solved them. The separating test (turn the box around; the planet's reading flips, the drift doesn't) is run daily at every airport gate and in every wellbore, and seventeen slides never mention the gyrocompass, the Foucault pendulum, or a century of ships steering by the signal the paper says was never detected.
Reviewed July 2026 · 32 pages · PPC · his first paper Reject

“The Atomic Clock Analysis and breakdown”

Argues atomic clocks measure “instrument behavior,” not time, so time dilation is “an interpretation, not a fact.” But it concedes the clocks differ and disputes only the label — and the clocks that agree run on unrelated physics: microwave cesium, optical strontium, gamma-ray Mössbauer nuclei, and the bare lifetime of a muon that has no dial at all. They report the same shift, including two clocks a millimetre apart on one shielded bench. His one serious objection — a shared “toroidal field” nudging every oscillator — dies where magnetism is excluded by construction: a muon in a 1.5-tesla ring (30,000× Earth's field) dilates by the pure relativistic number.
Reviewed July 2026 · 13 slides Reject

“An Audit of Terrestrial Gravity and Geodetic Standardizations”

Argues that standard gravity (9.80665 m/s²) is a “legal fiction” and that pendulums, triangulation, and vacuum chambers don't count as measurement. The 1901 history is real; the epistemology is engineered to exclude inconvenient instruments — including the worldwide network of absolute gravimeters that measure g by literally dropping a mirror in vacuum, which the paper never mentions.

The forces and the particles

Where gravity, the photon, the neutrino and the graviton are declared mathematical fictions — and answered by the thing that finds them: the neutrino detected in 1956 and now imaged in observatories, the Higgs found twice over in independent machines, gravity's inverse-square law confirmed in the lab to sub-millimetre range.

Reviewed July 2026 · 8 sections · Independent Research Reject

“A Critical Evaluation of Newtonian Mechanics, Field Dissipation, and the Fallacy of Subatomic Particles” (the “Newtonian Mechanics Dissection”)

Its showcase “undetectable invented particle” — the neutrino — was detected in 1956 and now has observatories that image the Sun and caught supernova 1987A: the whole method in miniature. The title says Newton; the middle third is quarks and the Higgs, welded to one fallacy: if science can't yet name a phenomenon's cause, the phenomenon is a “ghost.” But magnetism was measured, mapped and used for seven centuries before anyone could say what causes it — and the author doesn't doubt magnetism; his own model runs on “electromagnetic levitation.” Newton did the honest thing the paper claims to want — he stated the measurable law and refused to invent a mechanism (“I frame no hypotheses”) — and the paper faults him for exactly that restraint while inventing fields on every page.
Reviewed July 2026 · 23 pages · the “magnum opus” Reject

“A Critical Audit of Wave Propagation Mechanics and the Quantization of Gravity” (the graviton “magnum opus”)

His longest paper: waves don't really spread (the “fan” is just molecules catching the resonance), the graviton is a fiction, and gravity is a pressure gradient. But pump the air out of a vacuum chamber — his own §5 apparatus, on the workbench his §9 demands — and a lamp or radio inside still spreads as 1/r² with no molecules to catch anything; his favourite wave, the electromagnetic one, needs no medium. And to kill the spreading wave he breaks it into “thousands of straight-line rays” — a particle stream, the very thing he denies. Wave, or particle? He needs both. Even directed, a beam still thins as 1/r².
Reviewed July 2026 · 10 pages · Independent Research Reject

“The Functional Anatomy of Field Mechanics”

Casts Einstein as an anti-particle determinist and calls the photon “materially unrealistic” — but Einstein proposed the light-quantum in 1905, and the paper venerates E = hv while denying the very quantum that equation counts. It sells the Einstein-versus-quantum schism as knowledge “hidden from us”; it's the most-discussed controversy in a century of physics. Its grand alternative — “a particle is a knot in a field” — is quantum field theory almost verbatim, with the quantization deleted that explains the photoelectric effect, the STM, and every laser. And its tired-light cosmology dies on the supernova time dilation the author denied one paper earlier.
Reviewed July 2026 · Video · PPC v5.0 Reject

“A Comparative Analysis of Inverse-Square Scaling Constants”

Argues kₑ and G are “perfectly mirrored bookends” based on their dimensional formulas, with “down” supplied by the atmosphere's electric field. The formulas are correct; the inversion is SI bookkeeping that vanishes in Gaussian units, where kₑ = 1. The atmospheric-gradient mechanism fails the charged-balloon, thunderstorm, and Faraday-cage tests — the last using the author's own shielding principle from paper #2.

The shape of the world

The positive model — a stationary plane under a pressurized dome, with plasma luminaries on magnetic tracks — and the celestial mechanics it has to deny to hold together. The recurring problem: to hold the plane together, each paper has to deny a measurement its own sources depend on — and where the capstone offers a replacement for gravity, the pull it abolishes is hidden back inside it.

Reviewed July 2026 · 13 pages · PPC v5.0 · the capstone Reject

“A Mechanistic Audit of Classical Natural Philosophy” (the “Aristotelian Mechanics Dissection”)

The capstone manifesto — the whole model in one place: no gravity, only density and pressure, on a stationary dome-contained plane with plasma luminaries on maglev tracks, and time recast as “population management.” But both forces he offers in gravity's place have gravity hidden inside them: his own density-sorting is Archimedes' buoyancy, ρgV — delete the g and oil no longer separates from water — and atmospheric “downward pressure” is ρgh, the same g again. And the honest instruments still read a real pull: a dropped rock speeds up, you can measure the force on a spring scale, and it weakens with altitude — an acceleration his density has no term for.
Reviewed July 2026 · 11 slides Reject

“A Critical Examination of Earth's Physical Shape…”

Reject it, and the paper rejects itself: §2.2 states the oblateness “is real” and says satellite data confirm Earth “had always been” an oblate spheroid, while §7.3–7.4 call that same shape “not physically proven” and “empirically undetermined” — same author, same paper, opposite claims. Around that sit the familiar moves: it proves at length that Earth never physically morphed between sphere, spheroid, pear, and geoid — which is the mainstream position, verbatim — and its two live claims both fail, the mass-selective atmospheric leak falsifying dome containment and the orbital-instability argument refuted by the stable, precessing orbits of the very satellites in its own reference list.
Reviewed July 2026 · 9 slides · PPC v5.0 Reject

“A Critical Audit of Keplerian Mechanics and Foundational Optics”

Calls the elliptical orbit a “geometric abstraction” forced onto raw observation. But the ellipse is the observation — eight arcminutes of Tycho Brahe's data, which the paper itself calls hardware-grade, are what forced Kepler off the circle. The paper endorses the inverse-square law for light as “a verified, measurable fact” three slides after treating the identical law for gravity as fiction — though 1/r² is not a fact about light or gravity but about the geometry of space, shared by both. Its whole project is to undo the fall of epicycles — the very correction the author elsewhere holds up as science's model.
Reviewed July 2026 · Illustrated paper Reject

“A Critical Analysis of … Plant Structural Dynamics”

Argues plants have no sense of gravity — only soil-anchoring, hydraulics, and reaching for light. It concedes gravity loads a branch but denies it aims growth — and a seed planted upside down still roots down in dark soil; a clinostat and an orbiting centrifuge switch directional growth off and back on with no soil anywhere; and hydroponics grows correctly-oriented plants with no dirt to “clamp” anything. The paper attacks the 1806 experiment that already answered it.
About This Series

Why these papers get their own page

The Azimutha.FE account posts multi-slide photo carousels styled as formal academic papers: title pages, section headings, justified type, technical vocabulary. The formatting borrows the authority signals of published research while carrying none of its accountability — no named author, no checkable citations, no data, no DOI, and no hosted copy that can be searched or quoted. As of our reviews (searched July 2026), none of these documents could be found anywhere on the indexed web outside the carousels themselves.

The papers are also better than average for the genre: the historical footnotes are frequently accurate, the prose is fluent, and the arguments are structured. That makes them more persuasive than the usual fare — and more worth answering carefully. Our approach on every review is the same: steelman first, concede what's true, then check what remains against primary sources.

Standing invitation

If the author of these documents wants to respond, publish a citable copy, or correct anything on these pages — the contact form works, and unlike the papers under review, everything here has a fixed address. Corrections are applied publicly and credited.

Response Log

Exchanges with the author

19 July 2026 — first response received. Following publication of these reviews, the author posted a rebuttal as a comment thread on TikTok. We're logging it here as promised, in fairness summarized to its three arguments: (1) that this site "disproves physical experiments by running Python or JavaScript code" built on “mainstream data libraries” like NOAA and PySolar, which is circular; (2) that predictive math doesn't establish physical truth, citing Ptolemaic epicycles; and (3) that critics "cannot debunk from behind a keyboard" and bear the burden of physically replicating experiments.

Our reply, for the record: the three reviews he was rebutting contain no code — no Python, no JavaScript, no simulations, no PySolar, no NOAA calls. (Several of the later reviews, published since, include small interactive or animated figures — JavaScript or CSS that draws already-published numbers to scale; none of it generates any of the evidence.) Their evidence is physical experiment throughout: satellite free-fall comparisons of different materials, shielded torsion balances, gravimeters operating inside Faraday cages, commercial drilling outcomes, and a balloon test performable at home. Code does appear elsewhere on this site — solar-position work in the Kell analysis, geometry in the dome review — and the circularity question is answered for all of it on the method page; none of it appears in the reviews the rebuttal was aimed at. The Ptolemy point is good philosophy of science aimed backwards — Ptolemy's arrangement fell when a rival predicted what it could not (Venus's full phases; Kepler's ellipses then retired epicycles altogether), the reviews rest on exactly such differential tests, and PPC v5.0 had, as of the paper reviewed, put no number at risk. And the burden of physical proof has been met, in hardware, in the sources cited on every review, with volume and page numbers. The standing invitation stands: pick any single specific claim from any review and respond to it, and the response will be linked here verbatim. General objections to the review method itself are answered, for all reviews at once, here.

Method

How every review works

Each paper gets the same treatment, in the same order: provenance (where the document lives and what that implies), a steelman (the strongest honest version of its argument, with everything true conceded up front), a claim-by-claim audit with per-claim verdicts, the decisive counter-evidence, anticipated objections answered in advance, and a primary source list so nothing rests on our say-so. If you find an error, it will be corrected on the page, visibly.

The recurring objections to this method — circular code, “math isn’t physics,” keyboard replication, anonymity, and AI assistance — are answered once, for all reviews, at Objections to This Site’s Method, Answered.
